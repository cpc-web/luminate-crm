PATRICK MCGETTIGAN AND KATHERINE CAREY
Corporate Development
You've seen the logos on our signs and at our events, so you probably know: The Conservancy relies heavily on partnerships and generosity from corporate supporters — and Patrick McGettigan and Katherine Carey make it all happen. As CPC's brand new corporate development team, the pair manages relationships with about 85 existing corporate partners, while constantly looking for new sponsors. One of their biggest recent successes was getting BMW to contribute! Both Patrick and Katherine feel lucky to be representing the Conservancy and the Park.

"Central Park is so universally appreciated; there's nothing polarizing about our cause,” says Patrick. Katherine agrees: “Companies like to be associated with the CPC brand."The biggest draw for corporations who want to get involved is CPC’s Day in the Dirt program, a team-building exercise where about 30 employees from a company can come into the park for a day of weeding, raking, or planting. "We are so incredibly grateful to our field and volunteer staff for making Day in the Dirt happen," says Patrick. "Our corporate partners are always so impressed by our staff."

Both say the job is strategic, challenging, and creative, and they love building personal relationships. "It’s all about getting your foot in the door," says Katherine. Even CEOs have a memory of Central Park and are interested in helping!

Patrick is from New Hampshire and has been with CPC since April 2014. His first visit to Central Park was in 2005 on a trip with his family. The famous "Gates" art exhibit was up at the time, and he says, "There was no turning back!" He loves to hike in the North Woods with his dog, Crockett.

Katherine grew up in Connecticut and made many trips to Central Park as a kid. She remembers the park being a welcome respite from all the museums her parents dragged her to on their NYC outings! Katherine loves Central Park first thing in the morning and is known to go for a 6:30 am jog before work. She started her CPC career in October 2013.

Both work on the 8th floor at 60th Street; stop by and say hi!

4th Annual Ice Festival

This Valentine’s Day, bring your family to the 4th Annual Ice Festival Saturday, February 14, from 3:00 pm to 7:00 pm at the Naumburg Bandshell. Watch ice carvers from Okamoto Studio use electric chainsaws, chisels, and picks to transform 3,000 pounds of ice into a glistening replica of the Romeo and Juliet statue. As night falls, revel among colorful lights as the area becomes a 1980s-themed silent disco, all part of Central Park Conservancy’s 35th Anniversary celebration. The event is free.

CPC Crews Respond to Snowstorm









Snowstorm Juno may have been a bust, but Central Park Conservancy workers were ready for Mother Nature to bring it on. With weather forecasts calling for up to 30 inches of snow the night of Monday, January 26, the City shut down public transportation and parks. Conservancy offices were closed Tuesday, but 20 staff members reported for work to take care of the mere 9.8 inches of snow that did fall.

The Operations team cleared 2.5 million cubic feet of snow from Central Park pathways and perimeter sidewalks. Crews also assisted other parks, including Morningside, Marcus Garvey, and St. Nicholas Parks. Clockwise from top left, Jason Turnbull, Mark Tyson, and Brett Miller work in Central Park. At bottom, Kea Chea plows snow in Morningside Park. Photos by Sara Cedar Miller and Hannah Turner.

ART IN THE PARK

 A new art exhibit is coming to the southeast corner of Central Park. Desire Lines, by Paris-based artist Tatiana Trouvé, creates a link between the history of Doris C. Freedman Plaza and Central Park. The commission is composed of three massive custom-fabricated industrial shelving units containing 212 wooden spools wound with varying lengths and colors of rope. The dimensions of the ropes correspond to the exact lengths of 212 select paths that wind through Central Park, a number of which originate at the plaza, where Scholar’s Gate is one of the major entrances to the Park. The Public Art Fund will begin installing Desire Lines in late February, and it will be on view March 3 through August 30.

The Dirt...From Doug  

As we celebrate our 35th anniversary as a team, I have been reflecting on my own 30 years at the Conservancy. It was 1985 and my first project was the reconstruction of the Billy Johnson Playground. Little did I know then that it was just a sign of good things to come. I am proud to say that over the last 30 years we have improved all of Central Park’s playgrounds and are now restoring all of them once again, thanks to our incredibly successful Central Play campaign.

The design and care of Central Park’s playgrounds exemplifies our innovative approach to the care and maintenance of the park. From the newly restored 110th Street Playground to the work currently being done at Adventure Playground on 67th Street, each project is an example of excellence and our steadfast commitment to the communities we serve.
