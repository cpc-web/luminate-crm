SYLVIE ADAM and GARY RIBIS
Environmental Health and Safety
As every CPC employee knows (or should know!), safety is the most important part of the Conservancy Way, followed by courtesy, stewardship, and expertise. Sylvie Adam and Gary Ribis say their job is to promote that culture of safety. “We want to make it second nature,” says Gary.
The nature of work in the Park is physical, and tools such as leaf blowers and lawn mowers pose potential safety risks. “Our job is preventing injuries,” says Sylvie. This winter has presented special challenges, she says, because the unusually high amount of ice has resulted in a number of slips and falls.
She and Gary spend about 50 percent of their time at their East 104th Street office, and 50 percent in the field, usually traveling on bicycles marked with Conservancy flags. They conduct safety training of all kinds, and encourage staff members to report any potential safety problems before accidents happen. “We take the training to the staff when we can — for example, we’ll schedule it right after their lunch so they don’t have to go to another building. We pack up our props and go to them so it’s as convenient as possible,” says Gary.
“People don’t want to get hurt. Our job is to advise the staff on how to do things more safely,” says Sylvie. Both she and Gary enjoy interacting with the staff. They know workers in the Park are focused on getting the job done, and staff members appreciate knowing someone is looking out for their safety. “We’ve gotten a lot of positive feedback,” says Gary.
Gary grew up on Long Island and now lives in Tarrytown. He’s engaged to be married in September. He enjoys playing piano and is a big fan of Billy Joel. Sylvie, a native of Belgium, spent 21 years in Seattle before moving to New York. She now lives in Carroll Gardens, enjoys hiking in the Adirondacks — and used to play power forward on the Belgian national basketball team!

SPRING ART EXHIBIT DESIGNED TO CELEBRATE CPC’S 35TH ANNIVERSARY
 
A dreamy pathway of art will invite visitors to the north end of the Park this spring as part of CPC’s 35th Anniversary celebration. CPC has teamed with Creative Time, a nonprofit public art organization, to present Drifting in Daylight, a playful trail of performance and sensory experiences.
“We wanted to do something that celebrates the beauty of the Park and the surprises one can find wandering its paths, especially in the less explored northern end,” says Doug Blonsky.
“The exhibition pays tribute to the ingenious design of Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux in the mid-19th century,” says Cara Starke of Creative Time.
Drifting in Daylight will run Fridays and Saturdays from noon to 6 pm May 15 June 20. Visit creativetime.org for more information. Stand by for more details about the exhibit and how it will affect the Park.

    Quiz Answer: The Angel of the Waters statue, which sits atop Bethesda Fountain, is the only sculpture commissioned for the Park. It was created by Emma Stebbins and marked the first time a woman received a public art commission in New York City. Central Park Conservancy restored it in 1988.     The Dirt...From Doug  
    Well, here it is March already. While we’re all eager for spring, the relentless snowfall this winter makes me think about one of the things I am proudest of as I look back on the Conservancy’s 35 years: taking care of the park’s perimeter.
        
    It’s basically just the sidewalk — but you could also consider it the frame around this beautiful work of art that is Central Park. And that’s just how we treat it.
        
    It’s not particularly sexy, and no one really knows we do it, but day after day, in snow like we’ve been having, we are out there clearing more than 6 miles of perimeter pathway.
        
    Sometimes, our snow removal teams start at 4:00 am to get the job done before those first dog walkers, runners, and commuters head out to start their days. Just think about what the perimeter would look like if we didn’t take care of it in this tough weather!
        
    So if you’re one of the operations staff who’s been out there shoveling snow with frozen fingers once again, just remember you’re part of a proud tradition. For 35 years the Conservancy has tackled the maintenance issues large and small, every single day, in difficult conditions like these, because our goal is to make Central Park the most beautiful and inviting park in the world.
        
    And what could be a better invitation than a welcoming, safe perimeter?
        
    You’ve succeeded. Thank you.
